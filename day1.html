Day 1: Nice to meet you!

Day 1: Nice to meet you!



Script

Ken: Hajime mashite. はじめまして。

Henry: Hajime mashite. はじめまして。

Ken: Onamae wa nandesu ka. おなまえはなんですか。

Henry: Nandesu ka. なんですか。

Ken: Nihongo wa Wakarimasu ka. にほんごはわかりますか。

Ken: Iie, amari wakarimasen. いいえ、あまりわかりません。


Vocabulary
  • wakarimasu - わかります "I understand.""
  • ka - か, (particle) question
  • amari - あまり, (not) very much
  • wakarimasen - わかりません, "I don't understand"
  • hai はい - "yes", "that's right"
  • iie いいえ - "no", "that's wrong"
  • zenzen - ぜんぜん・全然, not at all (with negative verb)
  • yoku - よく, well, a lot, often
  • tokidoki - ときどき・時々, sometimes
  • shimasu - します, do, play
  • tabemasu - たべます・食べます, eat
  • nomimasu - のみます・飲みます, drink
  • tsukurimasu - つくります・作ります, make
  • norimasu - のります・乗ります, ride, get on
Grammar
1. Verbs, Non-Past, Formal, Affirmative and Negative

In Japanese, verbs occur at the end of a sentence, and they and can form a complete sentence on their own. Unlike English, subjects and objects are often omitted if it's obvious from the context. Formal verbs end with "-masu" (Affirmative, Present) and "-masen" (Negative, Present). "Present" here refers to actions done regularly or in the future. Formal speech is used with superiors (teachers, doctors, elders), strangers, or often between adults.

2. Hai / Iie: Affirming and Negating

"Hai" means "yes" regardless of the sentence's formality. Here are some examples:

    Wakarimasu ka. "Do you understand?"

    Hai, wakarimasu. "Yes, I understand."

Iie means "no" regardless of formality. Here are some examples:

  • Wakarimasu ka. "Do you understand?"
  • Iie, wakarimasen. "No, I don't understand."
3. Sentence Particles: Ka and Ne

Sentence particles like "ka" and "ne" are placed to sentences. Ka indicates a question. Here's an example:

  • Tabemasu. I eat it.
  • Tabemasu ka. Do you eat it?

Ne with falling intonation shows you assume the listener shares your feelings. It fosters a sense of shared understanding. Here are some examples:

  • Yoku nomimasu nee. "You drink a lot, don't you!"" (assumed agreement)
  • Wakarimasen nee. "We don't know, do we."" (checking for agreement)
  • Tabemasen ne? "You don't eat it, right?"" (confirmation)
4. Adverbs

Adverbs appear before verbs and indicate how much, how often, or how something happens. "Amari" and "Zenzen" with a negative verb show the degree to which something doesn't happen. Here are some examples:

  • Amari tabemasen. "I don't eat it very much."
  • Zenzen hanashimasen. "I do not speak it at all."

Yoku means "well," "a lot," or "frequently" depending on the context. Here are some examples:

  • Yoku wakarimasu. "I understand well."
  • Yoku kaimasu. "I buy it a lot/often."
