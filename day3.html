Day 3: Do you want to go?

Day 3: Do you want to go?

Script

Ken: Ikimasen ka? いきませんか?

Henry: Kyou wa chotto. きょうはちょっと。

Ken: Aa, sou desu ka. Ja, mata. ああ、そうですか。じゃあ、また。

Ken: Yamada-san wa? やまださんは？

Mr. Yamada: Watashi wa ikimasu yo. わたしはいきますよ。

Translation

Ken: Do you want to go? いきませんか。

Henry: Today, I can't. きょうはちょっと。

Ken: Oh, I see. Well then, next time. ああ、そうですか。じゃあ、また。

Ken: How about you, Mr. Yamada? やまださんは？

Mr. Yamada: I'm going. わたしはいきますよ。

Vocabulary
  • ikimasu いきます・行きます - go
  • ikimasen ka いきませんか・行きませんか - "Will you go?"
  • kyou きょう・今日 - today
  • chotto ちょっと - a little
  • sou そう - so
  • sou desu ka そうですか - Is that so.
  • Yamada やまだ・山田 - Yamada (surname)
  • watashi わたし・私 - I
  • yo よ - (particle, end of sentence)
  • boku ぼく・僕 - I (male)
  • ashita あした・明日 - tomorrow
  • asatte あさって・明後日 - the day after tomorrow
  • mainichi まいにち・毎日 - everyday
  • kimasu きます・来ます - come
  • kaerimasu かえります・帰ります - return, go home, come home
  • dekakemasu でかけます - go out
  • yasumimasu やすみます・休みます - rest, take time off
Grammar Notes
1. Negative questions as suggestions

Negative questions are sometimes used to suggest or invite to do certain actions:

  • Tabemasen ka. Won't you have some? / Why don't we eat? たべませんか・食べませんか？
  • Ikimasen ka. Won't you go? / Shall we go? 行きませんか？・い来ませんか？
2. Chotto: Impact Softener

Chotto literally means 'a little'. It is often used during a conversation when less-than favorable information is presented. For example, people often say chotto when rejecting an invitation or request, rather than saying no.

  • Ja, chotto itadakimasu. "Then, I'll take just a little." じゃ、ちょっといただきます。
  • Chotto wakarimasu "I understand a little." ちょっとわかります。
  • Suimasen. Chotto onegai-shimasu. "Excuse me. Can I ask a favor?" すいません。ちょっとおねがいします。
3. Aizuchi: How to be a Good Listener

When you participate in a Japanese conversation you are expected to give frequent feedback and show that you are engaged. Feedback includes nodding, making facial expressions, and using short expressions such as hai, soo desu ka, aaa, etc. These are called Aizuchi in Japanese.

Example: Aa, soo desu ka. "Oh is that so." ああ、そうですか。

4. Personal References

Watashi 'I' is the most common reference to "myself" in Japanese. Boku is used by male speakers, often by boys but not necessarily. Unlike English, where the pronoun you is used for the addressee in most cases, there are many ways to address and refer to others in Japanese. Last name + san is very common, but sensei (="teacher") and other titles are often used to address people in such positions.
